Diving through time

The flooded caves of Mt Gambier hide a rich megafauna history. Here's what it takes to find it.

By Meg M Walker, Joseph Monks and Julien Louys

Published: 20 Feb, 2024 10:08am

Sandwiched between Victoria's Great Ocean Road and the picturesque vineyards of South Australia's Coonawarra, underwater boneyards preserve some of Australia's extinct megafauna species in pristine conditions.

Accessing these fossils is no ordinary feat. They lie in deep, intricate, and sometimes very long underwater caves dotted along the appropriately named Limestone Coast. These caves sit beneath farms, roads and even the bustling city centre of Mount Gambier.

This tucked away corner of South Australia was once home to animals such as the diprotodon (giant wombat-like marsupial), thylacoleo (marsupial lion), and the sthenurines (short faced kangaroos).

The incredibly complete and intact fossils from the Mount Gambier underwater caves provide an avenue to start answering a fundamental question regarding the megafauna: what brought about their extinctions?

Understanding how and why megafauna species such as these are no longer with us led our research team to partner up with the Cave Divers Association of Australia (CDAA).

Over several expeditions, we’ve been building a picture of the sorts of species preserved underwater, and how they possibly could have gotten there.

While some megafauna bones were recovered by divers in the 70s and 80s, new scientific methods and diving technology means we can get at the important contextual information that surround the bones.

This includes samples that allow us to date the fossils accurately, and help us determine the environments that would have been present in the past.

But collecting these samples in an underwater environment without losing information poses a real technical challenge.

Exploration with cave divers

Cave diving is not for the fainthearted, and without proper training and adherence to guidelines, the pastime can be very risky.

The CDAA was founded in 1973 to establish and implement international standards for cave diving. Its members are highly experienced divers and instructors that assist in managing the risks of cave diving.

They have also been instrumental in fostering local relationships and finding many of the sites and fossils that we’re currently studying.

In 2022, our research team was awarded funding from the Australian Research Council that has allowed us to safely and systematically recover megafauna fossils with minimal loss of scientific information.

This represents the first efforts anywhere in the world to recover fossils by cave divers under scientific training and supervision from such complex systems.

The CDAA have also been training members of our team to dive safely within these cave environments.

There has been a true exchange of expertise between researchers and cavers, which in turn will help future divers understand the importance and heritage of the underwater landscape they explore.

Recording and collecting fossils from underwater caves, some over a kilometre through narrow passages, is a challenge. The typical equipment we use in dry caves, or even underwater archaeology, is simply not feasible.

Every collection technique has been retrofitted to work in these environments. Our tools must travel safely on a diver into the caves, and then work underwater.

We are using state of the art 3D recording to map the whole cave before we lift them out. Even collecting and drying the fossils has been considered, making sure they aren’t damaged by leaving their underwater bone bed.

Our initial investigations have yielded skulls and other bones from small marsupials, megafauna, dingos, and even a sheep or two. The bone beds have a long history of collecting animals, and this continues even today.

What’s next?

The enduring riddle of how Australian megafauna came to be extinct is one that will probably never be settled, but each piece of new data brings us closer to an answer. With the help of the CDAA, we are adding a piece to the puzzle which has never been really considered before.

Exploring these caves is about pushing the limits of our field. Building new techniques in underwater science and in the lab can be applied elsewhere in the world where amazing underwater fossils can be found.

In the meantime, we can keep raising awareness of why these places are so important. Without them, these historic, local stories are lost.

The caves are a wonderful resource and are spectacular, but we need to help preserve them while carefully bringing their secrets to the surface.
